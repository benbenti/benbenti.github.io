About me

I study vocal communication in animals. I am interested in the information content of animal sounds:
  • What kind of information is conveyed by animal signals?
  • How is this information encoded?
  • Can other individuals [conspecifics, competitors, prey/predator, parasites, ...] extract this information?
  • How do animals use information from their soundscape in decision-making?
In order to answer these questions, I describe the minute variations of animal signals and to associate them with the social, ecological, and behavioural contexts in which they are produced. I have experience working with different taxa (birds, primates, marine mammals) and using a wide range of recording equipment (animal-borne devices, passive acoustics, hand-held microphones) and acoustic analyses (audio-visual inspection, manual classification, supervised and unsupervised detection and classification algorithms).

Current project

I am currently involved in the COPIRA√èT project. This project explores the "social complexity hypothesis", which posits that the cognitive demands of social life are the main driver in the development of complex communication. Taking a comparative approach, we aim to describe social and vocal interactions in social and non-social corvids, but also in human and non-human primates. Based upon an existing multi-task neural network trained to locate and identify vocalising birds, Rookognise, we would like to adapt this architecture to new species, and to add a pipeline for individual and behaviour identification from video recordings.
